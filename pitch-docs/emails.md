a kid came around a corner on a scooter. fast. no warning.

dak lunged. i had him. but his teeth got close enough to the kid's leg that i still think about it. the kid was fine. dak was fine. but the gap between "fine" and "lawsuit + behavioral euthanasia order" was about six inches and zero seconds.

i went home and cried. then i ordered a basket muzzle the next day.

here's the part that surprised me:

once dak was conditioned to wear the muzzle — like, actually conditioned, not just tolerating it — he was a different dog on walks. calmer. less hypervigilant. like he could finally relax because the safety net was there, even if he didn't know that's what it was.

i could relax too. and dogs read us. so we both started having better walks.

the muzzle didn't fix his reactivity. that took two more years of training. but the muzzle is the thing that made the training possible at all, because i wasn't holding my breath every time we left the house.

people think muzzles are for "bad dogs." that's backwards.

muzzles are for owners who take their dog's safety — and other people's safety — seriously enough to put a tool on their dog that says: i'm in charge of what happens next.

tomorrow i'm going to send you the 5 most common things people get wrong about muzzles. some of them you've probably heard from a well-meaning friend. they're all wrong.

## EMAIL 3 — Education / myth-busting

**Send:** Day 4
**Subject:** the 5 things people get wrong about muzzles
**Preview text:** print this and hand it to anyone who side-eyes you at the park

---

hey {first_name},

every day i get the same comments under my muzzle posts. same five things. let's go.

**1. "muzzles are cruel."**

a basket muzzle that fits correctly lets your dog pant, drink, take treats, and yawn. cruel is letting your dog bite someone because you were too embarrassed to put a tool on them. cruel is your dog being put down because you didn't act early enough.

**2. "if my dog needs a muzzle, i should rehome them."**

nobody else is going to do a better job with your dog than you, if you put in the work. rehoming a reactive or bitey dog to "a farm" or a rescue is usually a death sentence in slow motion. the muzzle lets you keep working with the dog you have.

**3. "it'll make my dog more aggressive."**

a muzzled dog is a dog who knows they can't use their teeth. that knowledge often makes them calmer, not more frustrated. but only if they're conditioned to wear the muzzle. an unconditioned dog who feels trapped in something they hate WILL become more reactive. that's why protocol matters.

**4. "i'll just put it on when i need it."**

this is how you create a dog who panics at the sight of a muzzle for the rest of their life. muzzles get associated with the worst moments — vet emergencies, breaking up a fight, the moment you finally gave up. the conditioning has to happen in calm, boring contexts so the muzzle becomes neutral or positive before you ever need it.

**5. "people will judge me."**

some will. those people are wrong. the people who matter — your vet, your groomer, other dog owners who have been there, anyone who's ever managed a complicated dog — will respect you for it. and if you want a script for the rude ones, here you go: "thanks for your concern. she's safer this way." then walk away.

if your dog has any history at all that made you take the quiz earlier this week, you already know muzzle conditioning is something you should do. the only question is how.

tomorrow i'll send you exactly that.

— beck
