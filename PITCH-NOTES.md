# Pitch Notes — Conversation with Beck

## The opening text (send this first)

> Hey Beck, hope you and the new pup are doing well. Random thought — I've been building stuff with AI lately and I had an idea for something that might be useful for your business. No pressure, but if you're ever up for a coffee or a quick call I'd love to show you what I built. Either way, Bear says hi.

Don't pitch anything in the text. The goal is to get a meeting. The meeting is the pitch.

## The meeting setup

In-person coffee > video call > phone call. Want her looking at your laptop with you and able to take the quiz on her phone in real time.

Bring: laptop with the comparison page open, phone with the live quiz URL queued up.

Don't bring: a pitch deck, a contract, anything that looks like a formal sales process. She gets pitched constantly. The fact that you're bringing a working artifact, not slides, is the whole point.

## The actual pitch (in order)

**1. The setup (60 seconds).** "I've been thinking about your business since we did training with Bear. You have one of the biggest balanced trainer audiences on TikTok and your Gumroad products are basically invisible to all of them. I built something to show you what I mean."

**2. Show her the quiz on her phone (3-4 minutes).** Have her take it. Let her react to each screen without commentary. Watch where she pauses, what she comments on, what she fixes. Her edits are gold — write them down.

**3. Show her the landing page comparison on your laptop (3-4 minutes).** Walk through left side first (her current page — be respectful, not condescending), then right side. Point at the annotations below. Don't lecture. Let the comparison speak.

**4. Show her the email sequence (2-3 minutes).** Scroll through together. Highlight that it's built from her existing content themes — voice is hers, not made up.

**5. The ask.** "Here's what I want to do. I take your existing muzzle product, build this funnel around it, run it end-to-end. You don't write a new product. You keep doing what you're doing. I handle tech, copy, support, optimization — everything. We split revenue 75/25, you get the larger share. 90-day pilot. Either of us can walk after that."

**6. Three questions before either of you commits.**
   - What does the Gumroad storefront make in a typical month right now?
   - Is there anything in your Pet Station arrangement that would restrict you selling independently?
   - Are you willing to spend ~6 hours total over the first month — voice memos, copy review, recording 2-3 short videos for the product?

If she says yes to all three, you have a deal. If any are no, you have something to negotiate.

## Anticipated objections + responses

**"I tried digital products before, they didn't really work."**
Right — and the reason is the funnel, not the product. Your muzzle PDF is good. Nobody can find it. That's a 100% fixable problem and it's the whole thing I'm proposing to fix.

**"$47 is too expensive for a PDF."**
It's not a PDF, it's a protocol from a certified trainer. The market price for this kind of thing in the balanced training lane is $50-150. Larry Krohn's e-collar ebook is $20, Beckman's pro tracks are $1500. You're priced way below your credentials. Also — $47 is the launch price. We can A/B test pricing in month 2.

**"Why 25%? That seems like a lot for tech work."**
Because I'm not just doing tech work. I'm building the funnel, writing the copy, running the analytics, handling support, iterating monthly, building out the next products in the same backend. If I left, the system would atrophy in 90 days. That ongoing operator role is what the equity reflects. If you'd rather pay me a flat fee to build and walk, I can do that instead — but then it's a one-time project, not a partnership.

**"What about The Pet Station?"**
That's exactly why I want to know before we start. Worst case, we structure your business so the digital products are clearly under your personal brand and separate from anything that would conflict. We figure it out — but we figure it out before we build, not after.

**"I don't want to do live calls or coaching."**
Totally fine, this plan doesn't require any. You keep making TikToks. I run everything else.

**"Why you?"**
I built a CrossFit diagnostic app from scratch. I know how to ship products like this. And I know you and Bear, which means I actually care whether this works for you, not just whether it works for me.

## Things to NOT do

- Don't promise specific revenue numbers. Anyone giving her a specific number is lying. Talk in ranges if she pushes.
- Don't trash her existing Gumroad presentation. It's her work. The frame is "this is invisible," not "this is bad."
- Don't lock her into anything in the first conversation. The right ask is a verbal yes to a 90-day pilot, not a signed agreement.
- Don't bring up the second or third product yet. Muzzle only. Once we ship and learn, the next products become obvious.
- Don't oversell your dog knowledge. You don't have any. Your job is the platform, hers is the craft. Be honest about that — it's a strength, not a weakness, because it means you'll defer to her on all content decisions.

## After she says yes

1. Write up the deal in plain English — one page, not a legal contract. Both sign. Get a lawyer to formalize in month 2 if it's working.
2. Open a joint Stripe account or get her to add you to hers with a defined cut.
3. Set a kickoff session: get the voice memos, get the existing muzzle PDF assets, confirm the existing product description copy you can use.
4. Set a launch date 6-8 weeks out. Reverse-engineer the build calendar from there.
5. Build the production version of everything (the artifacts in this folder are pitch prototypes, not production code).

## After she says no

Don't be weird about it. Send a follow-up text: "All good — appreciate you hearing me out. The quiz is yours to use however you want if you ever change your mind." Then drop it. The partnership might come back in 6 months when she's tried her own thing and hit the same wall. Or it won't. Either way, you've learned how to pitch creator partnerships and you have working artifacts you can adapt for the next one.
